Executive Summary

Blackout Categories

4 COMPLETE BLACKOUTS
Why this matters A blackout isn't just a missed citation — it means LLMs have built a mental model of that category that doesn't include Applause at all. When a buyer asks ChatGPT about AI testing vendors, the model doesn't skip Applause — it genuinely doesn't associate Applause with AI testing. That's a training data and content gap, not a ranking problem. The fix is published, citable content that makes the argument explicitly.
🚫 AI Testing — 0/9 queries across all platforms P1 — High strategic value, fast-moving category

Who owns it: LLM evaluation tools (Arize, Braintrust, Galileo) and AI-augmented automation platforms (Tricentis, Mabl, LambdaTest). These tools handle automated testing of AI outputs and test automation for AI-adjacent workflows.

The LLM mental model: "AI testing" = tools that test AI systems programmatically. The category as LLMs understand it does not include human judgment applied to AI outputs — accuracy evaluation, naturalness, bias, edge case identification. Applause's entire human-in-the-loop differentiator is outside the frame.

The content argument: Automated tools evaluate AI outputs against defined rules. Humans evaluate AI outputs against real-world expectations. Those are different problems. A published piece that makes this distinction explicitly — with specific failure modes automation misses, and case study data from Applause's AI evaluation work — would insert Applause into a category that is currently moving fast and not yet calcified around any single vendor.

Urgency: This category is forming now. Competitors who publish citable content in the next 6 months will be harder to displace in 18 months.

🚫 Accessibility Testing — 0/9 queries across all platforms P1 — Clear differentiation argument available

Who owns it: Deque/axe appears in 9/9 queries. Level Access appears in 8/9. The category is dominated by automated scanner tooling and enterprise compliance services.

The LLM mental model: Accessibility testing = axe-core automated scanning + Level Access for compliance programs. The mental model is automated-first. Applause's human testing network — disabled users, real assistive technology, real-world conditions — is not registered as a distinct category of accessibility testing.

The content argument: Automated scanners catch approximately 30% of WCAG issues. Human testers find the rest. This is a documented, citable claim. A published piece making this argument explicitly — naming axe-core as complementary infrastructure that Applause works alongside, not competes with — positions Applause as the answer to the 70% that automation misses. Applause is not actually competing with Deque. It's filling the gap Deque leaves.

🚫 Mobile App Testing — 0/9 queries across all platforms P2 — Category needs explicit redefinition

Who owns it: BrowserStack (15+ appearances), Sauce Labs (8+), LambdaTest (6+). These are device infrastructure platforms — real device clouds and emulation environments.

The LLM mental model: Mobile app testing = device lab infrastructure. The question LLMs are answering is "how do I get access to devices?" not "how do I get real users on real devices in real conditions?" These are different questions with different answers. Applause's answer to the second question isn't registering because the second question isn't being asked in LLM responses.

The content argument: Device labs tell you whether your app renders correctly on 500 device configurations. Real-world testing tells you whether your app works for actual users on actual networks in actual locations. Content that explicitly names the failure modes device labs miss — carrier-specific behavior, real-world network degradation, actual user behavior patterns — creates a distinct category Applause can own.

🚫 Digital Quality — 0/9 queries across all platforms P1 — Applause's own brand term doesn't resolve to Applause

Who owns it: No single competitor owns this space — which makes it more alarming. LLMs route to ISO/DORA frameworks for definitional queries, Datadog for measurement queries, and enterprise QMS software for platform queries. Applause appears in none of these routings.

The LLM mental model: "Digital quality" is not a recognized vendor category. It routes to adjacent frameworks and tools depending on query intent. Applause uses this term as a brand identity but has not published the definitional content that would make LLMs associate the term with Applause.

The content argument: This is category creation, not optimization. A long-form, definitive piece — "What Digital Quality Actually Means and How to Measure It" — that defines the discipline, names specific metrics, references DORA and other frameworks, and positions Applause as the category leader is the foundational content investment. It needs to be specific enough to be citable and authoritative enough to displace the generic routing LLMs currently apply to this query.

Note: This is the only gap where there is no dominant competitor to displace. It's an open field. The brand that publishes the definitive piece first owns the category in LLM outputs.
