Overview

What the Persona Council does

The Persona Council is a Claude skill that assembles Applause's three primary buyer personas — Craig, Sam, and Alex — as a live critique panel. You hand it any marketing or sales asset: a one-pager, email, battlecard, landing page, deck, positioning statement, social copy, or even a single headline. The council reads it from each buyer's perspective and tells you exactly what lands, what falls flat, and what's missing.

It lives inside Claude as /persona-council. Trigger it with any plain-language request — no form to fill out, no template to manage.

🎭

Three distinct voices

Craig, Sam, and Alex each bring different priorities, objections, and language. You hear three real reactions, not a blended average.

⚡

Any asset, any stage

Works on rough drafts and polished final versions alike. The earlier you run the council, the cheaper the edits.

🎯

Actionable output

Each persona delivers a verdict, a confidence score, and specific suggested revisions — not generic feedback.

🔁

Iterative by design

Revise based on feedback and re-run the council. Converge on messaging that works before a human ever reads it.

Usage

How to use it — step by step

The skill is conversational. Paste your asset, describe the context, and the council goes to work. You don't need a special format — a plain-language request is enough.

1

Open Claude and trigger the skill

Start a conversation with a request that references the persona council, or invoke it directly. Include the asset you want reviewed — paste it inline, describe it, or share a link.

Example prompts

  • "Run this email through the persona council"
  • "What would Craig and Sam think of this one-pager?"
  • "Stress-test this positioning statement with our buyers"
  • "Persona council: review this landing page copy before I hand it to design"
2

The council reviews the asset

Each persona reads the asset from their own perspective. Craig flags anything that sounds hand-wavy or hard to validate. Sam looks for outcome language and user impact. Alex checks whether the operational reality holds up. You get three distinct reactions, not a consensus.

3

Review verdicts and revise

Each persona delivers a confidence score (1–10), their top objections, and specific suggested edits. Revise the asset based on what resonates, then re-run the council if you want to validate the changes.

4

Take it to market

Once the council signs off — or you've consciously decided to ignore their objections — you have buyer-tested messaging. Use it knowing what each segment will and won't respond to.

Output types

What the council produces

The council adapts its output to the asset type and your goal. Here's what to expect across the most common use cases.

📝

Full council review

All three personas respond in sequence. Each delivers a verdict, confidence score, top objections, and line-level suggested revisions.

🎯

Single-persona deep dive

Ask one persona to go deeper on a specific section, objection, or question. Useful for high-stakes deals where one buyer is the blockers.

🔄

Revised draft

Ask the council — or a single persona — to rewrite the asset incorporating their feedback. Iterate from there.

📊

Comparative scoring

Submit two versions of the same asset and get a side-by-side verdict. Useful for A/B decisions on subject lines, headlines, or positioning frames.

Tips

Getting the most out of it

🎯

Give context, not just content

Tell the council where this asset lives in the buyer journey and which persona is the primary audience. "This is a first-touch email for Craig" gives better feedback than a paste with no context.

🔁

Run it early, not just at the end

The council is most useful before you've committed to a direction — on a rough outline, a headline option, or a positioning frame. Waiting until final draft means more expensive revisions.

🔍

Ask follow-ups

"Craig, why exactly does that claim feel unsubstantiated?" gets more useful signal than accepting the first-pass critique at face value. Push each persona for specifics.

⚠️

Disagreements are the point

When Craig says one thing and Sam says the opposite, that's not a bug — it's the most useful output. It tells you where you have to make a deliberate choice about who you're writing for.

✏️

Use it to break ties

When two internal stakeholders disagree on copy, run both versions through the council. Buyer perspective is a more credible tiebreaker than personal preference.

📋

You don't have to take every note

The council reflects persona priorities, not absolute truth. If Alex pushes back on something you're deliberately writing for Sam, note it and move on. Context is yours to own.
